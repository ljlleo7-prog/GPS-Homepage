# GPS Community Economy Governance Policy

**Version**: Draft 1.0
**Date**: 2026-03-19
**Status**: Internal policy draft

---

## 1. Purpose

This policy defines how the GPS community economy should evolve without breaking trust with existing users.

The current website already has active users who care about the existing systems, especially:
- minigames
- driver betting
- wallet consistency
- the perceived fairness of TKN and REP

Because of that, this policy prioritizes:
- continuity
- anti-inflation discipline
- gradual change
- contribution-focused expansion
- low-backlash rollout

This is **not** a policy for replacing the current economy. It is a policy for governing its safe evolution.

---

## 2. Governance Objectives

The GPS community economy should:

1. preserve trust in existing balances and reward systems
2. avoid sudden inflation or devaluation
3. support community participation and contribution
4. reward meaningful effort more than shallow activity
5. keep legacy-familiar systems stable while adding new participation layers
6. ensure that any redesign is gradual, explainable, and reversible where possible

---

## 3. Protected Systems

The following are considered **protected systems** and must not be changed abruptly:

### 3.1 Wallet integrity
- Existing TKN balances must remain valid.
- Existing REP balances must remain valid.
- No reset, forced migration, or silent rebalance should occur.

### 3.2 Minigame economy
- Existing minigame reward loops should remain stable unless there is a severe exploit or operational issue.
- Monthly payout logic should not be diluted by major new uncapped token emissions.

### 3.3 Driver betting economy
- Driver betting should remain available and respected as a core community activity.
- Changes to its visibility, reward logic, or perceived importance must be gradual and clearly explained.

### 3.4 Historic effort protection
- Prior user effort must not feel invalidated by new policy.
- Users must not feel that previous participation became meaningless due to redesign.

---

## 4. Currency and Recognition Model

The community economy uses three distinct reward layers.

### 4.1 TKN
**Definition:** Scarce reward and support currency.

**Role:**
- preserve existing trusted reward loops
- support curated high-effort contribution rewards
- support optional project backing or funded bounty systems in the future

**TKN must not become:**
- a universal reward for all engagement
- a payment for low-effort participation
- an inflationary “activity farming” currency

### 4.2 REP
**Definition:** Trust, contribution, and community standing score.

**Role:**
- reflect reviewed, meaningful contribution
- reflect reliability and positive participation
- serve as a long-term indicator of community trust

**REP should be expanded more readily than TKN**, but still with discipline.

### 4.3 Non-economic recognition
**Definition:** Badges, roles, contribution history, project supporter markers, visible trust signals.

**Role:**
- encourage broad participation
- create belonging
- recognize effort without inflating TKN
- provide lightweight social rewards for users who are not yet deep contributors

This layer should be the default expansion path for new engagement systems.

---

## 5. Reward Policy

### 5.1 Default reward order
New engagement systems should reward users in this order:

1. **Badges / visibility / role recognition**
2. **REP**
3. **TKN**

TKN should be the most conservative reward layer.

### 5.2 Activities that should usually earn badges only
These actions are valuable for community belonging, but not strong enough for economic reward by default:
- joining a room
- introducing oneself
- attending an event
- liking/reacting to posts
- casual comments
- daily presence
- light recurring participation

### 5.3 Activities that may earn REP
REP may be awarded for:
- useful test reports
- structured product feedback
- issue reproduction and verification
- documentation contributions
- translation help
- community stewardship
- newcomer support
- project summaries and synthesis
- accepted mission completion

REP should be granted when a contribution is useful, reviewed, effortful, or trusted.

### 5.4 Activities that may earn TKN
TKN may be awarded only for:
- high-effort approved missions
- curated project bounties
- contributor campaigns with capped budgets
- support-pool funded work
- existing trusted reward loops already accepted by the community

TKN rewards must be limited, reviewed, and justified.

### 5.5 Activities that should not directly earn TKN
TKN should not be given directly for:
- likes
- ordinary comments
- posting volume
- low-effort engagement
- passive browsing
- repetitive low-value actions

---

## 6. Emission and Inflation Control

### 6.1 No uncapped expansion
No new TKN reward source may be introduced without a clear cap, budget, or review process.

### 6.2 Prefer funded rewards over fresh emissions
When possible, contribution rewards should come from:
- project support pools
- approved bounty allocations
- existing reserved budgets

This is safer than creating large new token emissions.

### 6.3 Monthly review requirement
Any new TKN reward mechanism should be reviewed monthly for:
- total token creation
- reward distribution fairness
- impact on minigame prestige
- impact on driver betting users
- community sentiment

### 6.4 REP discipline
REP should also be governed carefully.
REP inflation is less dangerous than TKN inflation, but still harmful if it makes trust meaningless.

REP should not become a generic activity counter.

---

## 7. Safe Evolution Policy

### 7.1 Add before replacing
New systems must initially be additive.

Preferred pattern:
- add new support mechanics
- add new contribution recognition
- add new mission types
- add new community structures

Avoid replacing legacy systems before users have adapted.

### 7.2 Parallel-track experimentation
If a system like bonds/markets is to be made more community-support oriented, the safer approach is:
- keep the old system intact
- launch a parallel support-style mechanism
- observe behavior before converging the models

### 7.3 No forced reinterpretation
Do not abruptly tell users that existing systems now mean something completely different.

Meaning should evolve gradually through:
- optional features
- soft reframing
- pilot programs
- visible continuity

### 7.4 Grandfathering principle
If thresholds, permissions, or trust systems evolve, existing engaged users should retain their previously earned standing where possible.

---

## 8. Community Contribution Policy

### 8.1 Strategic priority
The economy should increasingly support:
- project participation
- testing
- documentation
- translation
- design feedback
- stewardship
- building and helping

It should not over-prioritize shallow engagement.

### 8.2 Missions as the primary contribution ladder
Missions should become the main structured path from:
- follower
- participant
- tester
- helper
- contributor

Mission categories may include:
- testing missions
- documentation missions
- translation missions
- research missions
- stewardship missions
- build/contributor missions

### 8.3 Structured contribution formats
The platform should prefer structured contribution over vague interaction.
Examples:
- test report templates
- build logs
- issue reproduction forms
- proposal formats
- milestone support goals

This improves quality without requiring aggressive economic change.

## 9. Feature Governance Guidance

### 9.1 Minigames
- Keep current reward expectations stable.
- Use minigames as community rituals or events only after preserving their core value.
- Do not allow other reward expansions to trivialize minigame success.

### 9.2 Driver betting
- Treat driver betting as an established community ritual.
- Do not abruptly demote or obscure it.
- Any repositioning should be narrative first, mechanical later if ever needed.

### 9.3 Forum
- Improve structure and contribution quality first.
- Use thread types, project rooms, and prompts before changing economic incentives.
- Reward quality, not volume.

### 9.4 Missions
- Expand mission usefulness before expanding mission payouts.
- Mission quality control matters more than payout frequency.

### 9.5 Support markets / bonds
- If evolving toward donation/support style, do so through parallel optional systems first.
- Avoid rewriting current financial-style semantics overnight.

### 9.6 Wallet
- Wallet is a trust-sensitive interface.
- Avoid major semantic changes, new confusion, or visible instability.

---

## 10. Rollout and Communication Policy

### 10.1 No surprise economy changes
Any meaningful change that affects perceived value, progression, or fairness should be announced in advance.

### 10.2 Explain continuity clearly
Whenever new systems are introduced, communication should explicitly say:
- current balances remain valid
- current major activities remain supported
- new systems are additive first
- feedback will be collected before deeper changes

### 10.3 Pilot first
High-sensitivity changes should be piloted in one limited area before broader rollout.
Examples:
- one project support pool
- one new mission category
- one structured contribution flow
- one community recognition program

### 10.4 Review sentiment, not just metrics
Governance decisions should consider:
- user trust
- confusion
- perception of fairness
- attachment to existing systems

Not only raw activity numbers.

---

## 11. Decision Tests

Before approving any economy or engagement change, ask:

1. Does this change preserve trust for existing users?
2. Could this be perceived as inflation or devaluation?
3. Does this reward meaningful effort or just activity volume?
4. Can this be introduced additively rather than destructively?
5. Does this make project participation stronger without destabilizing core loops?
6. If users reacted negatively, could we pause or reverse the change safely?

If the answer to several of these is no, the change should not be rolled out yet.

---

## 12. Recommended Immediate Policy Actions

For the next stage of platform evolution, the recommended policy stance is:

1. keep minigames and driver betting stable
2. freeze disruptive wallet/economy redesigns
3. expand non-economic recognition first
4. use REP for reviewed contribution growth
5. keep TKN scarce and curated
6. build contribution architecture before deep economy reform
7. test support-style mechanics in parallel before replacing existing market-style systems

---

## 13. Summary

The GPS community economy should evolve carefully.

The platform already has users who care about the current system, and trust is more fragile than design ambition. The safe path is to preserve existing economic expectations while building a richer participation layer around them.

That means:
- keep the current trusted loops stable
- reward deeper contribution through structure and recognition first
- use REP more meaningfully
- keep TKN scarce
- migrate gradually, with communication and pilots

This policy exists to ensure that future redesigns strengthen the community without causing backlash, confusion, or loss of trust.
